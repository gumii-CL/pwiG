Project description

Introduction

A question of postcode, not biology

Rare diseases — pathologies affecting fewer than 5 in every 10,000 people — exhibit low individual prevalence but collectively represent a significant healthcare and social challenge. Millions of people worldwide live with these conditions, facing not only the difficulties inherent to the diseases themselves but also prolonged diagnostic journeys, limited therapeutic options, and a frequent lack of specialized information and resources.

In this context, newborn screening has become increasingly vital as a tool for early detection and intervention. However, its implementation remains inconsistent across different regions, creating inequalities in access to early diagnosis and, consequently, in opportunities for treatment and clinical follow-up.

To contribute to more accessible, rapid, and scalable genetic detection, Frida proposes developing a CRISPR-Cas-based biosensor capable of screening for rare diseases from a single biological sample. Our proposal seeks to offer an innovative alternative that facilitates the simultaneous identification of multiple genetic alterations, optimizing resources and reducing barriers to early diagnosis.

Motivation

Problem statement

Rare diseases represent a significant diagnostic and healthcare challenge: although each individual condition affects a small portion of the population, it is estimated that there are between 6,000 and 8,000 different rare diseases. Approximately 70% of these are genetic in origin, and many exhibit high mutational heterogeneity, making identification through conventional testing difficult. Patients often endure a “diagnostic odyssey,” facing delays that can span years, numerous consultations with specialists, and a battery of clinical and genetic tests before receiving a definitive diagnosis (EURORDIS, 2024).

This difficulty is compounded by regional inequalities in newborn screening programs and the exclusion of many conditions from these protocols, often due to a lack of specific treatments or detectable biomarkers. These factors directly impact the quality of life for patients and their families, increasing the psychological, social, and economic burden associated with these pathologies (Stark & Scott, 2023).

Landscape

Current strategies

In recent years, numerous national and European initiatives have been launched to improve the management of rare diseases. Biomedical research has paved the way for more precise diagnostic tools, specialized databases such as Orphanet, and international cooperation programs that connect researchers, clinicians, and patients. These advancements have fostered the development of orphan drugs, personalized medicine approaches, and novel gene therapies.

At the same time, organizations like EURORDIS and FEDER play a critical role in social awareness, advocacy, and the promotion of more inclusive public policies. In the diagnostic field, strengthening newborn screening programs through advanced genetic and genomic technologies represents a priority strategy for detecting pathologies at early stages, often even before the onset of symptoms.

Despite this progress, significant challenges remain regarding diagnostic delays, the high cost of testing, and inequalities in access to healthcare resources. Consequently, there is an ongoing need to bolster funding, improve coordination between health systems, and ensure more equitable access to diagnostic and therapeutic breakthroughs.

Innovation

Our proposal: Frida, a CRISPR-SHERLOCK screening platform

As our core proposal, this year our team introduces Frida, a biosensor capable of performing multiplexed genetic screening through the use of innovative CRISPR-Cas technology. This system allows us to use a neonatal DNA sample to identify whether a newborn carries a genetic variant responsible for a rare disease, utilizing a detection method coupled with fluorescence.

By developing a biosensor with these capabilities, we can move beyond screening for only a few specific conditions to screening for a wide selection of diseases and their variants. This is achieved through a system based on the sophisticated design of different combinations of these mutations. The ultimate goal is to achieve early, safe, and more efficient detection, aiming to improve both diagnostic outcomes and the quality of life for patients.

Technical overview

Components

How Frida detects a mutation

The core of our biosensor lies in CRISPR, a natural defense system found in bacteria and archaea that allows them to recognize and destroy invading genetic material, such as viruses or plasmids. It functions by storing DNA fragments from previous pathogens in CRISPR regions of the bacterial genome; these are subsequently transcribed into small guide RNAs (gRNAs) that direct associated proteins, such as Cas13, toward complementary genetic sequences to cut them with high specificity.

In biotechnology, this mechanism has been adapted into a highly precise tool for genetic editing and detection, enabling the programmable modification, deletion, or identification of specific DNA or RNA sequences.

In our project, we utilize a gRNA capable of recognizing the specific mutation causing a disease. When a patient carries that genetic variant, the CRISPR-Cas13 complex activates its collateral cleavage activity, cutting a reporter probe that generates a fluorescent signal.

By performing this assay in a multi-well plate using strategically placed guides according to a defined combinatorial logic, we can determine not only if a result is positive but also identify the specific genetic variant present. This distinction is vital, as symptoms and clinical outcomes often vary significantly depending on the specific mutation involved.

Frida’s digital companion: the gRNA Design App

Designing the right guide RNA is critical to the platform’s performance. To support this, we developed a web application for analyzing genetic mutations and generating optimized gRNA sequences for CRISPR applications. Researchers can input disease-related mutations in HGVS format, rsIDs, or OMIM codes, and the tool automatically retrieves the relevant genomic sequence and ranks candidate spacers.

Multiple input types

Accepts HGVS notation, rsIDs, and OMIM codes — covering standard ways to describe a disease variant.

Automated retrieval

Fetches genomic sequences from NCBI databases and applies the mutation to generate the target context.

Multi-metric ranking

Ranks candidates by GC content, homopolymer count, Bowtie off-target alignments, and ViennaRNA structure free energy.

Built with Blazor Server and an F# bioinformatics core, the app integrates the Bowtie aligner against the GRCh38 reference genome and ViennaRNA for RNA secondary structure prediction — making gRNA design reproducible, rigorous, and accessible to wet-lab researchers without computational backgrounds.

Detection: fluorescent probes

The detection of target sequences relies on the collateral activity of CRISPR-Cas13, a hallmark property of this RNA-guided nuclease. Once the crRNA specifically recognizes the target sequence, Cas13 is activated and gains the ability to non-specifically cleave nearby single-stranded RNA molecules.

Leveraging this mechanism, we incorporate fluorescent probes designed with a fluorophore and a quencher linked by a short RNA sequence. In the absence of activation, the proximity between the two keeps the signal suppressed (“dark”). However, when Cas13 identifies the target sequence and becomes active, it degrades these reporter probes, separating the fluorophore from the quencher and generating a detectable fluorescent signal.

In this way, the emission of fluorescence serves as a direct, rapid, and sensitive readout for the presence of genetic variants associated with the diseases under analysis.
